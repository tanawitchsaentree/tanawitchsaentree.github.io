## The problem

Every AI system fails. IDAS had no principled answer for what happened when it did.

When confidence was low, documents either stalled in a queue with no explanation, or were routed with false confidence to the wrong team. Operators discovered errors downstream — after someone had already acted on a bad classification, or after a document had sat unresolved long enough that it became an incident.

The system communicated one state: *classified*. It had no vocabulary for *uncertain*, *unrecognized*, or *confidence below threshold — human decision required*.

Operators weren't failing to catch errors. They had no signal that an error was possible.

Fallback isn't a failure message. It's a handoff. The interface had to communicate: *the AI processed this, here's what it found, here's why it's not routing automatically, here's what you need to decide.*

The difference between "error" and "escalation" matters — one asks the operator to fix something, the other asks them to own a decision.

## Trade-off ที่ยังอยู่

**Known failure modes only.** The fallback system catches cases where the AI knows it's uncertain — low confidence score, unrecognized document type. It doesn't catch cases where the AI is confidently wrong. A document classified at 0.92 confidence to the wrong category goes straight through. That's the harder problem, and it's still unsolved.

**Threshold is operator-agnostic.** The confidence threshold that triggers a fallback is set globally. Some operators are comfortable acting on a 0.7 confidence score; others want to review anything below 0.9. There's no per-team or per-operator calibration, which means the fallback boundary is a compromise that's slightly wrong for everyone.
