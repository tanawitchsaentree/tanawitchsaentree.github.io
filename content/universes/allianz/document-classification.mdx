## The problem

The platform processed thousands of incoming documents daily — claims, invoices, legal notices. The AI classified each one and routed it to a team. When it was wrong, no one found out until something downstream broke: a claim misrouted to legal, an invoice sitting in the wrong queue for days.

When it was right, handlers still double-checked everything. Not because they distrusted the system — because the system gave them no signal about when *not* to.

The cost wasn't bad AI. It was that good AI and bad AI looked identical at the point of decision.

## What I considered

- **Option A:** Show a single confidence percentage on each classification — cut because a raw number (e.g. "78%") has no context for what 78% means in practice. One team's acceptable threshold is another team's review-everything zone. A number without calibration teaches nothing.

- **Option B:** Color-code results (green / yellow / red) based on a fixed threshold — cut because a fixed threshold treats all document types the same. A claim mis-classified at 0.65 confidence is a different risk than an invoice at 0.65. Color became noise fast.

- **Option C (chosen):** A tiered display that de-emphasizes confidence on high-confidence routes — score present but visually recessive, not the lead element. Below threshold, a focused review card takes over: the category the AI chose, the closest alternative it considered, and a one-line explanation of why it was uncertain. The question it answered wasn't "what's the score?" — it was "do I need to check this?"

## The decision

Confidence information is a cost, not a feature. Every time a handler reads a number, interprets it, and decides it's fine — that's wasted attention for a case that didn't need review.

The interface de-emphasized confidence on high-confidence routes — score present, but visually recessive, not the lead element. Below threshold, a review card replaced the standard result: classification chosen, closest alternative, brief reasoning. Two actions: *confirm route* or *reclassify*. The score moved to the background. The decision moved to the front.

The underlying rule: surface uncertainty only when it requires a decision. Everything else should be invisible.

## What changed

The review card became the only place uncertainty existed. Everywhere else, the system either ran or stayed silent.

Configurators started asking "why did this hit uncertain?" instead of "why was this wrong" — the framing shifted from blame to diagnosis, which changed how they used the prompt management tool downstream.

**Confidence thresholds are static.** Every document type uses the same review threshold. A legal notice and an invoice carry different downstream costs when misclassified — the threshold should reflect that, but calibrating per-type requires historical volume by document category that didn't exist at launch.

**No audit trail for the confidence state.** The display optimises for the handler in the moment. Someone reviewing a routing decision six months later has no record of what confidence score triggered which route. That's a gap that will matter if a classification is ever challenged in a compliance context.
